Agentic AI

Agentic AI refers to systems designed to act autonimously and with goal directed behavior. Instead of waiting for explicit commands from a person, an agentic AI can plan tasks, make decisions, and coordinate actions across tools or environments. It evaluates context, adapts to feedback, and works with minimal supervision.

Impact of agentic AI on most of Canadian society

Agentic AI is reshaping everyday life in Canada by transforming major public services. In healthcare, it automates triage, scheduling, and paperwork, helping reduce wait times and easing pressure on staff. Government services become faster and more accessible because AI handles tax inquiries, immigration files, and benefits processing. These changes streamline essential systems and influence nearly all Canadians.

Computer Vision

Computer vision is a branch of AI that enables machines to interpret and analyze visual information from images or video. It powers technologies like facial recognition, robotics, and automated inspection.

Impact of computer vision on the world economy

One major impact on the world economy is increased industrial efficiency. computer vision systems can detect defects, monitor production lines, and optimize logistics, lowering costs and boosting global manufacturing productivity.

Industrial Internet of Things (IoT)

The Industrial Internet of Things connects machines, sensors, and software across factories, energy grids, and transportation systems. These connected devices collect data, enable automation, maintenance, and more efficient operations. IoT reduces downtime, improves safety, and supports data driven decision making. It transforms industries by increasing productivity, lowering costs, and enabling interconnected infrastructure.

Impact of IoT on a minority group in Canada

For Canadians with disabilities working in industrial environments IIoT can improve accessibility and safety. Smart sensors can detect hazards, automate heavy tasks. Remote monitoring technologies also open new employment opportunities for individuals who cannot perform physically demanding roles, allowing them to contribute through supervisory or data analysis positions.

Edge computing

Edge computing processes data closer to where it is generated. on local devices, sensors, or nearby servers rather than sending everything to distant cloud centers. This reduces latency, improves reliability, and allows real time decision making. Its essential for applications like autonomous vehicles, smart factories, and remote monitoring.

Impact of edge computing on developing societies

Edge computing accelerates development by enabling real time, low latency digital services in regions with weak connectivity. improving transportation, healthcare, agriculture, and overall digital inclusion.

Green Technology

Green technology refers to tools, systems, and innovations designed to reduce environmental harm and support sustainable development. It includes renewable energy (solar, wind, hydro), electric vehicles, energy efficient buildings, recycling systems, and pollution reduction technologies. The goal is to lower emissions, conserve resources, and create cleaner, more sustainable societies.

Positive impacts

Green technology reduces greenhouse gas emissions, improves air quality, and protects ecosystems. In Canada, renewable energy sources like hydro and wind help lower national emissions. Globally, green tech supports climate change mitigation and reduces reliance on fossil fuels, helping countries transition toward cleaner energy systems.

Green technology creates new industries, jobs, and investment opportunities. In Canada, green tech companies contribute billions to the economy and support high skilled employment in engineering, manufacturing, and research. Worldwide, the green tech sector is one of the fastest growing markets, driving innovation and attracting global investment.

Negative impacts

Many green technologies such as solar panels, electric vehicles, and energy efficient systems have high upfront costs. In Canada, wealthier urban areas adopt green tech faster than rural or low income communities. Globally, developing countries often struggle to afford large scale green infrastructure without international support.

Producing green technologies requires minerals like lithium, cobalt, and rare earth metals. Mining these materials can harm ecosystems and create social challenges, especially in countries where mining conditions are unsafe or poorly regulated. This creates a global tension between sustainability goals and the environmental cost of producing green tech.
