1. Extended Reality (XR)

Extended reality, often called XR, includes technologies like virtual reality, augmented reality, and mixed reality that blend digital elements with the real world. According to the TechTarget article on extended reality, these tools are already being used in areas such as education, healthcare training, and entertainment. For example, professionals can practice difficult tasks in realistic virtual simulations before performing them in real life. For many Canadians, XR could make learning and job training more engaging and safer. However, XR equipment like headsets can still be expensive, meaning not everyone may have equal access to these experiences.

Source: TechTarget - What is Extended Reality

2. Brain Computer Interfaces (BCI)

Brain computer interfaces, or BCIs, allow the brain to communicate directly with a computer. The PMC research article on BCIs and communication disabilities explains that this technology can help people who have severe movement disorders such as ALS patients, individuals with spinal cord injuries, and people with locked in syndrome. BCIs can translate brain signals into commands so a person can type messages or control assistive devices. For someone who has lost the ability to communicate, this technology could restore independence and connection with others. However, these systems are still complex and expensive, which means access may currently be limited.

Source: PMC - Brain Computer Interfaces and Communication Disabilities

3. Multimodal User Interfaces

Multimodal user interfaces allow people to interact with technology using different input methods such as voice, touch, gestures, and visual feedback. The ScienceDirect article on multimodal interfaces explains that combining multiple interaction methods can make technology easier and more natural to use. In developing societies such as India, Kenya, Bangladesh, and rural areas across Africa, smartphones are often the main way people connect to digital services. Voice commands or gesture controls can help people who may have limited literacy or little experience with traditional computers. While this technology could improve access to information and education, it still depends on stable internet connections and modern devices.

Source: ScienceDirect - Multimodal User Interfaces Research

4. Post Quantum Cryptography

Post quantum cryptography focuses on creating encryption systems that will remain secure even when powerful quantum computers are developed. According to the NIST article on post quantum cryptography, today's encryption protects things like online banking, private communication, and government data. However, future quantum computers may be able to break current encryption methods. Researchers are working on new cryptographic algorithms designed to resist these threats. This technology is important for the world economy because global banking, e commerce, and digital communications depend on secure data. Updating systems worldwide will require time and investment but will help protect the global digital economy.

Source: NIST - Post Quantum Cryptography FAQ
